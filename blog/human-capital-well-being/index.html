Navigating the Micro-Level: How Management Structures and External Shocks Shape Employee Well-being

Employee well-being is not only a social outcome. It is a micro-level signal of organizational resilience, workplace design, and internal governance quality.

Much of sustainable development focuses on macro-level policy, corporate disclosure, and financial market outcomes. Yet the true engine of any organization is its people. Recent evidence shows that global emotional stress has continued to worsen, underscoring the universal need for healthier work and living environments (Piao et al., 2024).

The key question is how external macroeconomic crises interact with internal management structures to influence psychological well-being. By shifting attention to the micro-level, we can see how resilient organizational designs help buffer employees against unprecedented disruptions.

External shocks are uneven

External shocks such as the COVID-19 pandemic do not affect all workers equally. Employees across sectors experienced severe and highly heterogeneous occupational stress. Front-line workers and employees in vulnerable sectors, especially medical, retail, and accommodation industries, faced some of the sharpest increases in psychological burden (Piao et al., 2022b; Xie et al., 2023).

This heterogeneity matters. It shows that an employee's vulnerability to external shocks is shaped not only by the shock itself, but also by pre-existing workplace conditions. Where job demands are high and workplace resources are weak, macro-level crises can quickly become individual-level distress.

Workplace resources create resilience

How can companies protect their workforce from these pressures? The answer lies in the careful structuring of workplace resources. Data-driven typologies of individually perceived workplace environments reveal that simply reducing workload is not enough. Organizations also need to enrich the resources available to employees.

Job autonomy, strong organizational support, fair rewards, and better workplace relationships can buffer high job demands and improve well-being (Xie et al., 2025). These typologies also vary across demographic groups, which means that occupational stress and resource needs differ by gender and age. A resilient workplace is therefore not a one-size-fits-all design. It is a system that recognizes different forms of vulnerability and support.

Management structure is the driver

The ultimate driver of workplace resources is internal governance. Corporate ESG engagement and socially responsible activities can directly relieve occupational stress by fostering a more supportive work environment (Piao et al., 2022a). But governance also operates through the composition and behavior of management teams.

Female managers often serve as important agents of organizational change. Through leadership styles that encourage open communication, participative learning, and team support, they can improve workplace resources at the task, group, and worksite levels. These resources strengthen work engagement, improve team cohesiveness, and reduce employees' psychological burden (Xie et al., 2024).

Micro foundations of sustainable firms

Employee well-being is not merely a byproduct of external conditions. It is shaped by internal governance, workplace resources, and managerial choices. Organizations that cultivate diverse, resource-rich, and supportive management structures are better positioned to withstand future shocks.

In this sense, human capital is not separate from sustainability. It is one of sustainability's micro foundations. A firm that protects and develops its people is not only doing good for employees. It is building the organizational resilience needed to navigate uncertain social, economic, and environmental change.
