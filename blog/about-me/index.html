About My Research

Why doing good is also good business and good for people: a look at the questions that drive my work across ESG, corporate governance, and human well-being.

Why do some companies thrive by being responsible, while others treat sustainability as a checkbox? And what happens to the people inside those companies, day to day, when governance is strong or weak?

These are the questions that have shaped my research for nearly a decade. My work sits at the intersection of finance, management, and public policy, but at its core, it is about one idea: economic value and social good are not opposites. They can, and should, reinforce each other.

My research organizes around five themes, each approaching this idea from a different angle.

01 · ESG and capital markets

A central finding across my work is that ESG is not just a reputational exercise. It has measurable financial consequences. Companies with stronger ESG practices reduce information asymmetry in markets, which in turn reduces equity misvaluation. Investors increasingly recognize this: failing to address materially relevant environmental risks raises a firm's risk premium. The message is clear: sustainability disclosure is also risk disclosure.

02 · Climate strategy and corporate transformation

As climate policy tightens globally, how companies respond strategically matters enormously. My research shows that green mergers and acquisitions, acquiring clean technology or divesting carbon-heavy assets, can help buffer policy shocks while generating market value. Companies that proactively align with frameworks like TCFD also benefit from lower costs of debt and equity. Climate ambition, done well, is financially rewarded.

03 · Workplace environments and mental health

Behind every company are people, and their well-being is often invisible in aggregate financial data. My research tracks how macroeconomic shocks like COVID-19 created severe, unequal stress across sectors, particularly in healthcare, retail, and hospitality. But the data also reveal something hopeful: workplaces that offer autonomy, organizational support, and inclusive leadership, including female managers, measurably reduce stress and improve engagement.

Good governance does not stop at the boardroom. It shapes the daily experience of every employee.

04 · Board characteristics and internal governance

Who sits in the boardroom shapes what a company becomes. My work finds that board gender diversity is not merely a social target. It directly encourages proactive environmental strategies and improves long-term firm value. In SMEs, the personal traits of top executives, their openness, conscientiousness, and risk tolerance, leave fingerprints on firm strategy in ways that do not show up in large-firm governance models.

05 · Macro sustainability and public policy

Individual firms do not operate in a vacuum. Local government SDG initiatives create spillover effects on corporate behavior in their regions. National-level inclusive wealth, accounting for human, natural, and produced capital, amplifies the returns to ESG investment. Sustainability policy is not separate from financial performance; it is the background condition that determines what good governance is worth.

Looking ahead

My goal is to build bridges across these levels: connecting what happens to an individual employee, to what a boardroom decides, to how a city or nation accounts for its own wealth. I believe empirical research, done carefully and honestly, can give policymakers, investors, and business leaders the evidence they need to make a more resilient and human-centered economy possible.
