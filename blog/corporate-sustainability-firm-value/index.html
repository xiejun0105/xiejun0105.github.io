ESG & Capital Markets · Essay

Decoding the Value of Sustainability: How ESG Practices and Financial Materiality Shape Capital Markets

ESG is no longer a peripheral public relations tool. It has become a core information channel through which firms, investors, and markets negotiate sustainable value.

The rise of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) criteria has changed how investors and researchers evaluate corporate success. High-quality ESG practices and transparent disclosures do more than build reputation. They can improve intrinsic corporate value, operational efficiency, and the quality of information available to capital markets.

Recent evidence shows that ESG engagement can reduce information asymmetry and help correct equity misvaluation, allowing stock prices to reflect firms' fundamentals more accurately (Wang et al., 2026). At the same time, comparable ESG metrics are necessary for markets to price sustainable value and social equity in a consistent way (Keeley et al., 2022).

Disclosure is not always linear

The assumption that "more ESG disclosure is always better" is too simple. Corporate transparency regarding ESG information can have a complex, nonlinear relationship with corporate efficiency. Xie et al. (2019) show that corporate efficiency peaks at a moderate level of ESG disclosure, rather than at either low or high extremes.

Too little disclosure increases information asymmetry and makes it difficult for investors to evaluate a firm's sustainability profile. Excessive disclosure, however, may expose unfavorable information, create unnecessary compliance costs, or distract managerial attention from substantive performance improvements. The question is therefore not only how much a firm discloses, but whether disclosure is useful, credible, and decision-relevant.

Materiality changes the question

This leads to a central evolution in sustainable finance: financial materiality. Not all ESG issues affect a company's bottom line in the same way. Investors increasingly evaluate ESG information through industry-specific materiality frameworks, such as SASB metrics, to identify environmental risks and opportunities that are genuinely relevant to future cash flows and risk exposure.

From this perspective, excellent overall environmental performance is not enough. What matters is whether firms manage the environmental issues that are financially material for their specific industries. Evidence on environmental materiality shows that materially relevant performance can improve financial outcomes such as return on assets, while also lowering the cost of equity and debt capital.

Markets price material environmental risk

Capital markets are increasingly attentive to these material environmental risks. Xie et al. (2023) show that firms failing to engage in financially material environmental management face meaningful exposure to systematic risk. Their research documents pricing anomalies related to environmental risk and suggests that investors demand higher risk premia when firms perform poorly on material environmental issues.

From responsibility to financial signal

The practical implication is clear: ESG is no longer just a public-facing responsibility framework. It is also a financial signal. To create durable value, companies need to focus on substantive, materially relevant environmental and social performance rather than treating disclosure as an end in itself.

For investors, this means looking beyond aggregate ESG scores and asking which sustainability dimensions are financially material. For managers, it means aligning disclosure with real operational improvements. For researchers, it means continuing to test where, when, and how sustainability information becomes priced by markets.
