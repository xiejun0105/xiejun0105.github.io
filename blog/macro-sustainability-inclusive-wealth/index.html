Macro Sustainability & Inclusive Wealth · Essay

Beyond the Firm: How Macro-Sustainability and Inclusive Wealth Drive ESG Returns

Corporate ESG performance does not operate in a vacuum. Its financial value is shaped by regional institutions, public policy, and the wealth foundations of society.

To understand sustainable development, we need to look beyond individual companies and examine the broader macro-sustainability landscape. A region's institutional environment and fundamental wealth act as the soil from which corporate sustainability grows.

This perspective matters because firms do not implement ESG practices in isolation. They operate within communities, policy systems, labor markets, education systems, health systems, and natural environments. The same corporate sustainability action can therefore produce different financial and social outcomes depending on where it takes place.

Human capital as macro infrastructure

Comprehensive sustainability assessment requires accounting frameworks that include human capital, not only produced assets or short-term economic output. Population dynamics, education, and health are central to this accounting because they determine the long-term productive and social capacity of a region (Chen et al., 2025).

Tracking human capital development globally reveals significant regional disparities. Some regions accumulate human capital through balanced investments in education, health, and economic participation, while others face structural constraints that limit long-term wealth creation. These differences create the macro conditions under which firms compete, hire, innovate, and invest.

Public policy shapes firm behavior

Macro-level institutional environments influence corporate behavior through both formal policy and local social expectations. Public policies and local government initiatives targeting the Sustainable Development Goals create positive spillover effects on the social sustainability practices of regional firms (Ida et al., 2024).

Companies adapt to the sustainability preferences, regulatory pressures, and institutional norms of their local communities. This same multidimensional logic can also be used to evaluate public-facing institutions. For example, hospital sustainability can be assessed through an integrated index that combines ESG and digital transformation, showing how sector-specific organizations can be evaluated through broader sustainability infrastructure (Takeda et al., 2025).

Inclusive wealth moderates ESG returns

How does this macro environment affect the bottom line of corporate ESG practices? One answer lies in Inclusive Wealth, a comprehensive measure that includes human, natural, and produced capital. A region's Inclusive Wealth acts as a macro-level moderator that can amplify the financial returns of corporate ESG practices (Xie et al., 2025).

In regions with abundant human and natural capital, corporate environmental efforts can translate more strongly into positive financial outcomes. These structural conditions mean that identical ESG practices may be rewarded differently across countries and regions. ESG returns are therefore not only a function of firm-level effort. They also depend on the wealth base and institutional environment surrounding the firm.

Why context changes strategy

The implication is that corporate ESG performance cannot be interpreted as a universal input with a universal return. Regional public policy, social infrastructure, human capital, and natural capital all influence whether sustainability practices become financially valuable.

For policymakers, this means that strengthening regional wealth foundations can increase the returns to corporate sustainability. For investors, it means that ESG evaluation should consider the macro context in which firms operate. For managers, it means that sustainability strategy should be aligned with local institutional conditions rather than copied mechanically across markets.

By connecting corporate actions with regional wealth structures, we can better understand how ESG can drive both firm profitability and global sustainable development.
